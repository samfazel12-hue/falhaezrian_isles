## Overview

The Age of the Bloodroyals was the long era in which the children and descendants of [[Béorn]] and [[Rosaria]] dominated [[Sentra]] and much of the wider [[Falhaezrian Isles]].

It followed the unification of Sentra and the disappearance of Béorn. During this age, the founder lines established houses, seats, domains, temples, castles, ports, schools, military traditions, magical inheritances, cadet branches, and territorial claims.

The age ended gradually. Its political logic broke down as Bloodroyal lines multiplied, rival claims hardened, and the eldest Brandt line tried to turn founder seniority into ruling principle. The final collapse came through the 112-year [[Bloodroyal War]].

## The Bloodroyals

The Bloodroyals, also called the Seed of Béorn, are the direct children of [[Béorn]] and [[Rosaria]].

Their father sent them forth from [[Jeor’s Hall]] beneath [[Kacore]] to establish their own houses, seats, domains, temples, institutions, and lineages.

## Political Order During the Age

The Age of the Bloodroyals did not have the later constitutional shape of the High Kingship.

Power rested in founder descent, local seats, military strength, sacred custody, magical inheritance, and negotiated hierarchy among Bloodroyal houses.

| Political Element | Function |
|---|---|
| Founder prestige | Descent from Béorn and Rosaria gave legitimacy. |
| House seats | Castles, towns, forests, islands, and temples became political bases. |
| Cadet branches | Younger lines extended influence and created rival claims. |
| Sacred institutions | Temples, libraries, and founder sites preserved authority. |
| Military houses | Some lines ruled by conquest, gates, passes, and fortifications. |
| Craft houses | Forge and material control became political power. |
| Local law | Many regions retained their own customs under Bloodroyal dominance. |
| Marriage and descent | Intermarriage multiplied claims and inheritance disputes. |
| Personal longevity | Older Bloodroyals remained politically active across generations. |

| Institutional consequence | Magical inheritance helped justify house authority, priesthoods, schools, and arcane orders. |
| Political danger | Magical lineage created legitimacy that ordinary law could not easily erase. |

## Longevity and Dynastic Density

Bloodroyal longevity shaped the whole era.

| Consequence | Effect |
|---|---|
| Long founder lives | Older children of Béorn remained politically active long after ordinary generations passed. |
| Many descendants | Houses produced dense cadet branches. |
| Slow succession | Seats could remain under one person for unusually long periods. |
| Delayed crises | Disputes could simmer for centuries before becoming open war. |
| Overlapping claims | Living elders, grandchildren, and cadet branches all had plausible rights. |
| Historical compression | Later people treated centuries of activity as one founder-shadow. |

## Causes of Later Collapse

| Cause | Effect |
|---|---|
| Too many legitimate lines | Founder descent became too widely distributed to settle hierarchy cleanly. |
| Eldest-line ideology | Brandt descendants turned seniority into a political claim. |
| Long-lived rulers | Old claims remained active for too long. |
| Cadet branch ambition | Younger lines gained real power and resisted subordination. |
| Sacred competition | Temples, libraries, and holy sites carried political authority. |
| Military autonomy | Houses with castles, passes, ports, and armies could resist central settlement. |
| Regional divergence | Western, southern, mountain, and island regions did not share one political culture. |
| Weak succession law | No stable mechanism existed to prevent dynastic overreach. |
| External pressure | Yarrowgold and later Continental threats exploited internal disorder. |

## Relationship to House of Béorn

The later [[House of Béorn]] inherited the legitimacy of Béorn’s order but changed its structure.

| Bloodroyal Age | Later House of Béorn |
|---|---|
| Founder descent central | Service, office, merit, and recognition also central. |
| Houses dominate locally | Councils and ministries mediate power. |
| Seniority contested | Succession requires institutional confirmation. |
| Blood prestige high | Blood prestige limited by postwar order. |
| Personal lines rule | Governing order outlives individual branches. |
| Cadet branches multiply claims | Councils restrain claims. |

## Relationship to the Bloodroyal War

The [[Bloodroyal War]] was the end product of the age.

| Bloodroyal-Age Problem | War Expression |
|---|---|
| Brandt seniority | Brandt claimant asserts eldest-born right. |
| Founder-line overgrowth | Houses split, shift, and fight. |
| Weak succession logic | Civil war becomes solution mechanism. |
| Military autonomy | Samwise Wilgenfal rises through command. |
| Sacred and territorial claims | Houses fight over seats and legitimacy. |
| External vulnerability | [[Yarrowgold]] intervenes amphibiously. |
| Exhaustion | People and houses accept a new High King. |

## End of the Age

The Age of the Bloodroyals did not end in one clean moment.

It ended through long decline, internal fragmentation, and final war.

| Marker | Meaning |
|---|---|
| c. [[500 BHK]] | Approximate end of dominant Bloodroyal age. |
| [[112 BHK]] | Bloodroyal War begins. |
| [[0 AHK]] | Samwise crowned High King; new dating begins. |
| Brandt defeat | Eldest-line supremacy rejected. |
| Wilgenfal rise | Military legitimacy and institutional recognition replace pure founder seniority. |
| Council order | Later constitutional settlement grows from the war’s aftermath. |
