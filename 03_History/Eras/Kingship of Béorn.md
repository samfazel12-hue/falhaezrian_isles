## Overview

The Kingship of Béorn was the first unified rule over [[Sentra]].

It began after [[Béorn]] defeated [[Krova Kingkiller Yarrowgold]] at the [[Siege of Twixtriver]] and was proclaimed King of Sentra in [[2099 BHK]].

The reign lasted until Béorn’s disappearance in [[2064 BHK]]. It created the political, military, sacred, and institutional foundation later claimed by the [[Bloodroyals]], [[House of Béorn]], the [[High King]], and the councils of the realm.

## Definition

The Kingship of Béorn was not yet the later [[High Kingship]] of the [[Falhaezrian Isles]].

It was a Sentran kingship: a rule over the unified island of [[Sentra]], built from conquest, alliance, sacred marriage, personal command, and the incorporation of older regional powers.

Later institutions treated it as the root of all lawful House authority.

## Political Structure

Béorn’s kingship was personal and founder-centred.

The later councils, ministries, and House offices did not yet exist in their mature form. Rule depended on Béorn’s own authority, battlefield legitimacy, sacred status through Rosaria, negotiated submission, and the loyalty or containment of regional rulers.

| Structure | Function |
|---|---|
| King’s command | Direct rule through Béorn’s authority. |
| War companions  | Military followers and early captains. |
| Regional submissions | Defeated or allied rulers brought under the new kingship. |
| Local laws | Many older regional customs likely survived. |
| Sacred legitimacy | Rosaria and Kacore gave the kingship more than military force. |
| Founder household | Early root of later Bloodroyal and House structures. |
| Oath network | Personal allegiance to Béorn before later institutionalized House law. |
| Tribute and service | Practical submission through taxes, warriors, roads, stores, and hostages . |

## Unification Policy

Béorn’s rule did not make Sentra culturally uniform.

The kingship likely used different methods in different regions.

| Method | Likely Use |
|---|---|
| Conquest | Against rival kings, warlords, and hostile strongholds. |
| Alliance | With useful local rulers, towns, and military powers. |
| Marriage and hostage ties  | To stabilize defeated houses and border regions. |
| Religious settlement | To avoid revolt in Ignahuatl and older sacred regions. |
| Trade protection | To bring old cities such as Rhodokra into the new order. |
| Road and pass control | To make unity practical beyond proclamation. |
| Tribute | To bind regions without direct administration everywhere. |
| Oath-taking | To convert enemies into vassals or bound allies. |
| Founder grants | To reward followers and prepare later Bloodroyal expansion. |

## Religion and Legitimacy

Béorn’s kingship rested on both victory and sacred recognition.

His relationship with [[Rosaria]] gave the kingship a supernatural foundation. His victory over Krova gave it military legitimacy. His unification of Sentra gave it practical authority.

## The Bloodroyal Household

The Bloodroyals were born before and during the period around Béorn’s unification and kingship.

## Disappearance of Béorn

Béorn disappeared in [[2064 BHK]].

His disappearance ended the founder reign and left the new kingdom with an unresolved problem: the authority had been unified by one extraordinary figure, but no ordinary mechanism yet existed to replace him.

| Consequence | Notes |
|---|---|
| End of personal kingship | The founder king no longer held the settlement together directly. |
| Bloodroyal uncertainty | His children and descendants inherited prestige but not a settled hierarchy. |
| Rise of founder lines | Bloodroyals became the main political actors of the next era. |
| Institutional gap | Later councils and House law grew from the need to stabilize succession. |
| Mythic ambiguity | Disappearance allows religious, political, and magical interpretations. |
