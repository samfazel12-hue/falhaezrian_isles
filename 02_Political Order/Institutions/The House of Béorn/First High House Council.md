**Institution type:** Council
**Seat:** [[Béornic]] land in the north
**Members:** 88
**Voting threshold:** 45 yay votes
**Affiliation:** [[House of Béorn]]

## Overview

The First High House Council is the smaller and more selective of the two great chambers of the Falhaezrian state. It stands close to the crown, the old House order, and the long memory of the realm. Its purpose is not to drive daily government, but to preserve continuity, approve major decisions, restrain sudden political movement, and advise the The [[High King]].

## Function

The First House serves as the highest chamber of approval, counsel, and constitutional memory. It receives laws drafted by the [[Second High House Council]] and may approve, reject, or return them. It also advises the The [[High King]] on long-term policy, succession, dynastic continuity, and the interpretation of custom.

The First House does not usually originate the main body of law. Its power lies in approval, refusal, delay, and legitimacy.

## Membership

The First House has 88 members. They are not elected directly and may not openly run for office. Members are chosen, approached, and asked to accept elevation.

Selection is made from across the Isles through the [[Second High House Council]], though no formal regional quota exists. Historically this made the First House heavily Sentran, [[Béornic]], and eastern in character, though it is now somewhat broader than it once was.

Membership is held for life unless ended by death, retirement, incapacity, or formal judicial expulsion.

## Removal

A member can be stripped of rank only through grave legal proceedings. Normal political disagreement is not enough.

Grounds for removal include:

- corruption
- treason
- gross abuse of office
- conduct judged incompatible with the dignity of the House

## Political Character

The First House is narrow, durable, cautious, and insulated from public swings. It is built to resist volatility. This makes it useful as a stabilizing force, but also makes it a natural enemy of reformers, populists, and ambitious ministers.

Where the Second House gives motion to the realm, the First House gives restraint.

## Relation to the [[High King]]

The First House is one of the chief institutional checks around the crown. A reigning The [[High King]] may name an heir, but the appointment does not become fully lawful by crown will alone. The candidate must first be approved by the First House and then confirmed by the [[Second High House Council]].

In wartime, the [[High King]]’s emergency powers can overshadow the councils, but the First House still matters because it controls legitimacy, continuity, and formal approval.

## Relation to the Second House

The First House depends on the Second House for selection of new members, but also restrains the Second House by reviewing its laws.

This creates built-in tension. The Second House is broader, elected, ministerial, and politically active. The First House is smaller, older, slower, and harder to move. Their conflict is not a flaw in the system. It is part of the design.

## Strengths

- Preserves institutional memory.
- Prevents sudden factional overreach.
- Gives succession legal weight beyond bloodline.
- Shields the state from short-term political panic.
- Keeps old House authority alive without making rule purely hereditary.

## Weaknesses

- Can block necessary reform.
- Can become dominated by old eastern elites.
- Can outlive the political reality it claims to guard.
- Can create resentment among elected ministers.
- Can appear detached from the outer Isles, especially the west.

## Key Connections

**Works with:** The [[High King]], [[Second High House Council]], [[House of Béorn]]
**In tension with:** reformist ministers, regional blocs, peace factions
**Depends on:** constitutional custom, council legitimacy, House prestige
**Restrains:** sudden legislation, unstable succession, regional agitation

## Adventure Use

- A candidate for [[High King]] needs First House approval.
- A First House member is accused of corruption or treason.
- Reformers try to force the chamber to accept wider representation.
- A war measure passes the Second House but stalls in the First.
- A council elder knows an older legal interpretation that could undermine the crown.
